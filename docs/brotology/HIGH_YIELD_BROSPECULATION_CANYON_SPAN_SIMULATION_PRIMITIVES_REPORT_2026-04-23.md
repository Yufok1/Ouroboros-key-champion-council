## Domino Correction

The `domino` picture is useful only if translated correctly.

The big outer panels can absolutely be the visible end members on the swivels.

But the tiny sequencer should not be treated as the true motor.

The honest relation is:

- `micro_sequencer` = trigger, latch trip, timing release
- `charged_member` = the big preloaded rotating body
- `outer_panel` = the visible fast expression
- `catch_and_return` = the next stage that captures, redirects, or reloads the cycle

So the correct causal chain is:

1. preload a heavy member with real stored energy
2. use a small sequencer to release it at the right instant
3. let the large member throw phase into the next member
4. catch, damp, or recharge before the next cycle

That means the tiny element may look like it "tips" the big one,
but really it is releasing a loaded mechanism.

## One Truth Spine Rule

Per `coordinated_acceleration_soluble_surfaces`, do not create two controller realities.

For this facility:

- truth spine = bodies, constraints, mass, load, angle, envelope, service state
- acceleration carrier = phase/stroke logic over the relay chain
- soluble surfaces = panels, skins, visual trails, heat bands, dashboard layers

That means:

- visible outer panel spectacle is downstream
- the inner structural state remains authoritative
- the dashboard cannot silently become the mechanics engine

## Recommended First Facility

The first simulation facility should be:

- `canyon_span_slice`

Shape:

- 2.5D cross-section
- one basin
- two rim anchors
- one primary span
- one main pivot
- three to seven relay segments
- one counterweight shaft
- one outer panel family
- one lower service band

Why this is the right first slice:

- it proves the motion grammar without pretending whole-city fidelity
- it makes charge, crest, return, and rebound visible
- it exposes whether the outer panel wave is real or fake
- it gives the observation layer something small enough to measure honestly

## Stage-Zero Demonstrator

Before the canyon-scale facility, there should be a smaller public proof:

- `momentum_lobby_demonstrator`

This is the airport-lobby machine version:

- obviously kinetic
- visibly charged
- precise about timing
- able to route or fling bounded payloads
- spectacular enough that a spectator understands it instantly

What it proves:

- trigger vs prime mover
- catch/release discipline
- timing accuracy
- preload and recharge behavior
- public perception value

What it should move:

- bounded demonstration masses
- utility payload analogs
- routed capsules, bins, or process blocks

What it should not do:

- throw uncontrolled random loads
- rely on visual chaos as proof of engineering
- skip capture and recovery lanes

This precursor matters because it earns the larger architecture.

## Suggested Controller Profiles

The first controller profiles should be:

1. `charge_hold_release`
   Load the inner mass, hold, then release into the chain.
2. `crest_and_return`
   Test whether the outer member reaches the top and how it descends.
3. `switchback_relay`
   Route energy or mass between terraces/shelves before the outer release.
4. `storm_brace`
   Freeze or damp the system under high external forcing.
5. `maintenance_transfer`
   Route one panel/module into the lower service band.
6. `lobby_fling_precision`
   Launch and recover a bounded payload through a visibly exact inertia cycle.

## Utility Justification

The heat source becomes high-yield only when it is tied to real sinks.

Good sinks:

- habitation heat
- hot water
- drying
- absorption cooling support
- water lifting / pumping assist
- milling / grinding / crushing
- sterilization / waste handling
- process routing in the lower service bands

Bad sink:

- pure spectacle with no plant logic underneath

So the useful combined read is:

- the plant charges part of the mechanism
- the mechanism moves the structure
- the same plant also feeds real habitat/service needs

That is much stronger than a decorative hot core.
